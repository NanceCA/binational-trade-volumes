Understanding Binational Trade Volumes

Using machine learning to explore trends in binational trade and mobility between Mexico and the US.

A capstone project for the Deloitte Consulting Machine Learning Guild

About the project

International commerce can generate a variety of favorable social, economic, and political externalities. In addition to mutual economic gain, trade can also encourage a strengthened social and political bilateral relationship between trading partners.

Recognizing that international trade can often also trigger unfavorable circumstances, this study will focus on better understanding and optimizing the favorable components of a binational commerce relationship. This moves forward under the assumption that the broader economic system will continue to be globalized and so finding ways to optimize the positive externalities is a worthwhile effort for improved political, social and economic dynamics around the world.

Mexico and the United States make ideal selections for this study given the intricate historical trade relationship and the interwoven dynamic in the areas of culture, business and politics. For comparative purposes, trade and mobility data is included for the US-Canadian relationship.

This capstone project aims to answer the following key questions through exploratory data analysis, data preprocessing and regression.

What are we trying to understand?

  • Trade volumes are often a way to quantify a bilateral relationship. If mobility between countries can be a catalyst for a strengthed bilateral relationship (and trade volumes an indicator of an existing relationship), can key datapoints around trade volumes provide any insight into mobility trends? In other words, is there a relationship between mobility data and binational trade data?
  • Can insights in trade and mobility trends support the institutions that facilitate efficiency in both areas? Let's take California ports of entry as a use case.
  • How can better understanding the nuances in the trade data (aggregate import and export volumes) and mobility data provide greater insight into the results of binational commerce?
  • Can binational travel, that may or may not come as a requirement of binational transactions, also be an indictator strengthened cultural mutual understanding?

Ready? Let's go!

About the datasets:

Dataset Source Key Variables
1) General Exports from US to Mexico US International Trade Commission (USITC) Domestic Exports: Aggregate measure of goods grown, produced, or manufactured in the U.S. This includes commodities of foreign origin that have been changed in the US
FAS Export Value: The Free Alongside Ship value is the value of the exports at the U.S. port based on the transaction price
Observances: Aggregate value between 1996-2019
2) General Imports from Mexico to the US US International Trade Commission (USITC) General Imports: The value of the total physical arrivals of merchandise from foreign countries
General Customs Value: The price actually paid or payable for the merchandise excluding U.S. import duties, freight, insurance and other charges
Observances: Aggregate value between 1996-2019
3) US-Mex Border Ports of Entry U.S. Department of Transportation (DOT) Port Name: Lists 27 unique ports of entry along the US Mexican Border. See EDA for the complete list.
Measure: Describes the way in which the travel was conducted. There are 12 unique values including: trucks, bus passengers, pedestrians
Value: The recorded number of individuals who traveled through the port name via the measure
Observances: 353,962 mobility records for US-MX and US-Can border between 1996-2019

Disclaimer: The original intent was to quantify the most comprehensive snapshot of total binational travel. This includes airport ports of entry in the United States as well as the value of those travelers that visit Mexico from the United States on an annual aggregate level. Unfortunately, reliable data on these data points was not readily available. This project may be updated with these additional datapoints, if available. Stay tuned and check back periodically!

Models

Let's correlate the three variables using multiple linear regression and polynomial regression to better understand the nuances and extract value from the trends.

Models Variables Reason
Simple Linear Regression Displayed below to understand export and import trends over time, and get insight on the correlation between the two variables. Both export and import data are linear in nature. Fitting these series on a linear regression model can provide a general sense of the pattern.
Polynomial Regression Relating import and mobility data as well as export and mobility data. Polynomial regression works on any size of dataset, and works great for nonlinear patterns. See visualization below to see the bias/variance tradeoff and the polynomial degree chosen for the model.
Naive Bayes and Logistic Regression Relating import, export, and port of entry data to make predictions on which California ports of entry may experience higher mobility volumes to help them build efficiencies in facilitating travel. Helpful to make the business case more concrete, by helping identify which ports of entry can facilitate mobility and trade.

Naive Bayes

Plotting Mobility by Port Code to understand which mobility observances are more likely to travel through California ports of entry.


Identifying the Ports of Entry in CA

The boolean value represents whether or not a mobility observance happened to be through a California port of entry. Running a Naive Bayes model including the Port Code and Mobility enables us to predict which port codes may experience higher travel transactions (commercial and non-commercial) as mobility rises. Equipped with this information ports of entry can cater to the final user and dedicate resources to design for efficiencies.

See the Jupyter Notebook

Additional Naive Bayes and Logistic Regression models were conducted to predict the specific measure (or means of transportation) for binational mobility. This included commercial and non-commercial measures. The top measures were Pedestrian travel and Commercial Truck travel. The model visualizations can be found on the Jupyter Notebook. No significant trends were identified to pursue insights on a specific port code. More granularity on each of the measures is needed.

Insights and Next Steps

There's so much more to explore. This is the first step in trying to understand the quantified relationship among countries in close proximity.


Insights:


  • Q: Is there a relationship between mobility data and binational trade data?

    A: The polynomial regression suggests that a relationship exists. Given the polynomial regression, it is clear that other factors (many of them may be social and political) influence mobility aside from just trade volumes. An interesting comparison emerges when taking a look at the US-Canadian trade data which shows 1) similar trade data trends compared to that of US-MX but 2) the mobility patterns are more consistent. This may further showcase how other factors influence (inhibit or enable) mobility as well.
  • Q: Can insights in trade and mobility trends support the institutions that facilitate efficiency in both areas?

    A: Yes! The Naive Bayes model reveals how likely it is that mobility is focused in California ports of entry according to select features. Chambers of Commerce, local and federal government, as well as private sector actors that depend on trade can leverage these data points to cater efficiency and an improved user experience to busy port of entries in California.
  • Q: How can better understanding the nuances in the trade data (aggregate import and export volumes) and mobility data provide greater insight into the results of binational commerce?

    A: More data about the influence of mobility may be necessary to best answer the question above. However, using the information and regression models here, the inflection point in the polynomial regression by export/import model may provide further input into the result of binational commerce. Furthermore, the simple linear regression further suggests that import and export volumes are closely related and positively inclined, further linking both economies.

  • Q: Can binational travel, that may or may not come as a requirement of binational transactions, also be an indictator strengthened cultural mutual understanding?

    A: To best answer this question, binational or perspective/sentiment surveys should be correlated with the period after the inflection point in mobility.


Next Steps:

  • Incorporate the multivariate backwards elimination technique with the mobility data to build a port of entry predictive model. Extract the specific ports and mobility numbers from the Naive Bayes model.
  • Continue searching for comprehensive mobility datasets that include airports as port of entry for binational visitors.
